# Competitor Site Review — Agent Team Notes

Reviewed from the perspective of Lincoln Mitchell's agent team. Each note identifies what Lincoln should learn, steal, or avoid.

---

## 1. bradfrost.com — Brad Frost

**Background:** Author of Atomic Design. Design systems educator, speaker, consultant. Runs Southleft with Ian Frost and TJ Pitre. Ships open-source tools (FigmaLint, Story UI, design-systems-mcp). Based in Pittsburgh, PA.

### Agent Notes

**Content Strategist** | 🟢 GOOD | Hero
Brad's hero: "I make things and help people." Six words. Immediately clear what he does and who he serves. Lincoln's hero requires multiple sections before the value proposition lands. Brad proves you can be a thought leader and still lead with simplicity.

**Content Strategist** | 🔴 CRITICAL | Overall positioning
Brad positions as practitioner AND educator — the book, the course, the blog, the consulting. Four revenue streams, all visible from the homepage. Lincoln has one: hire me. Brad's site says "I have so much to offer that you'd be lucky to get my time." Lincoln's says "Please consider me." The posture is fundamentally different and it affects how recruiters and hiring managers perceive authority.

**Visual Designer** | 🟡 IMPROVE | Visual design
Cream background (#f6f5de), dark buttons (#32373c), warm photography. The palette feels human and approachable — not corporate, not startup. Lincoln's dark editorial theme (#0f172a) is striking but cold. Brad's site feels like sitting down for coffee. Lincoln's feels like a keynote stage. Both are valid, but Brad's converts better for the "let's talk" CTA.

**UI Designer** | 🟢 GOOD | Navigation
Simple top nav: Course, Training/Workshops, Book, Blog, Contact. Five items. Each is a product or conversion point. Lincoln's nav has more pages but several are conceptual (/thoughts, /system) rather than actionable. Brad's navigation IS his business model.

**Content Strategist** | 🟡 IMPROVE | Blog/Content
Brad's blog is the engine. 20 posts visible, mixed format (essays, links, personal, multimedia). "Words that would otherwise be coming out of my mouth." The blog IS the thought leadership — the site is just the frame. Lincoln's /thoughts pages are excellent content but positioned as philosophy rather than an active blog. No RSS. No "latest thinking" feed on the homepage. Brad's content feels alive; Lincoln's feels curated.

**Stakeholder Simulator** | 🔴 CRITICAL | Social proof
Brad lists Bluesky, LinkedIn, Mastodon, Threads, YouTube, Instagram, GitHub, Codepen, Spotify, Last.fm. The breadth signals "this person is everywhere, actively creating." Lincoln has LinkedIn and GitHub. The social footprint gap makes Brad look like a community leader and Lincoln look like a job seeker.

**UX Researcher** | 🟡 IMPROVE | Multiple products
Course (Design Tokens), Book (Atomic Design), Consulting, Blog — four pillars. Each reinforces the others. Lincoln has the knowledge to create a course or book (the /thoughts content is already structured like chapters). The absence of any productised offering means Lincoln competes only on hourly rate, not on authority.

## 2. jansix.at — Jan Six

**Background:** Principal Product Designer at GitHub. Co-creator of Tokens Studio. Shipped AGENTS.md router pattern, Cabana design kit. Speaker at IDS 2026. Designer-developer hybrid.

### Agent Notes

**UI Designer** | 🟢 GOOD | Hero
"Hi, I'm Jan Six. A product designer / developer hybrid with a strong interest in empowering creatives & makers." One sentence, clear identity, clear mission. No jargon. Lincoln's equivalent requires scrolling past the I<N>C diagram. Jan proves that hybrid identity can be stated simply.

**Content Strategist** | 🔴 CRITICAL | Positioning simplicity
Jan's entire brand: "designer / developer hybrid who empowers creatives." That's it. Everything flows from this — the plugins, the design kits, the talks. Lincoln's positioning requires understanding INC, NorthStar Prototyping, Agentic Design Systems, the career arc, the cognitive framework. Jan's is immediately hireable. Lincoln's is immediately impressive but takes work to parse.

**Visual Designer** | 🟡 IMPROVE | Visual design
Monochromatic, minimal, content-first. No decoration. Placeholder SVGs instead of hero images. The restraint signals confidence — "the work speaks." Lincoln's site has more visual polish but also more to process. Jan's loads faster cognitively.

**UI Designer** | 🟢 GOOD | Products section
Six Figma plugins shown as a grid: tokens, batch styling, grid automation, scaling, asset generation, wireframe blocks. Each is a shipped artefact. This is the strongest signal a design systems person can send: "I don't just talk about systems, I build tools that other designers use." Lincoln's FigJam plugin exists but is buried. The design-system.html page is a differentiator but positioned as documentation, not as a product.

**UX Designer** | 🟡 IMPROVE | Work showcase
Three projects: Cabana, Platoyo, Websnap. Minimal descriptions, heavy reliance on visuals. Effective because the GitHub and Tokens Studio credibility does the selling. Lincoln's work page shows more projects but with activity descriptions rather than outcomes — as the content review already identified.

**Stakeholder Simulator** | 🟢 GOOD | Conversion
Mailing list signup: "Sign up and get informed about updates & new launches." Email, Instagram, Twitter, GitHub in footer. Jan collects an audience. Lincoln doesn't. Every visitor to Lincoln's site who doesn't hire him is lost forever.

**Motion Designer** | 🟢 GOOD | Performance
No animations. No transitions. Fast load. The absence of motion is a design choice that says "I respect your time." Lincoln's motion design (stagger animations, orchestration) is well-executed but adds cognitive overhead that Jan avoids entirely.

## 5. dianawolosin.com — Diana Wolosin

**Background:** Senior UX Designer, Indeed. Figma San Diego Leader. IDS 2026 speaker on Machine-Readable Design Systems for MCP and LLMs. 8-configuration MCP benchmark, 4389 prototypes.

### Agent Notes

**Visual Designer** | 🟢 GOOD | Dark theme execution
Black background (#000000), white text, purple accents (#A37DB7, #9660F6). Glassmorphism, gradient overlays, glow effects. The dark theme is more dramatically executed than Lincoln's — deeper blacks, more intentional accent colour. Diana's purple is her brand. Lincoln's blue/purple/cyan is pleasant but not as ownable.

**Motion Designer** | 🔴 CRITICAL | Animation sophistication
Typing animation in hero. Staggered fade-ins with translateY. Scale transforms on hover (0.96-1.07). Scroll-triggered pinning. Marquee text. Custom cursor. Flickity carousel. The motion design is dense and intentional — every interaction communicates "I care about craft." Lincoln's motion (stagger, orchestration) is good but less varied. Diana's site feels like a motion design portfolio in addition to a DS portfolio.

**UI Designer** | 🟡 IMPROVE | Typography system
Three font families in distinct roles: RobotoMono for headlines, Roboto for body, Satoshi Variable for accents. Responsive scaling from 72px desktop to 24px mobile. The typographic hierarchy is more deliberately designed than Lincoln's system font stack. It signals: "I make intentional typographic decisions."

**Content Strategist** | 🟢 GOOD | Data-driven positioning
Diana's IDS talk featured hard numbers: JSON is 93% accurate vs MDX at 75%. JSON costs $300/year vs MDX at $1,500. 4,389 prototypes tested. Lincoln's site has no equivalent metrics. The numbers make Diana's expertise falsifiable and therefore credible. Lincoln's claims ("one-person innovation engine") are unfalsifiable and therefore less convincing to technical audiences.

**Stakeholder Simulator** | 🟡 IMPROVE | Corporate backing
Diana works at Indeed — enterprise credibility by association. Her benchmark was run against Indeed's real component library (@indeed/ifl-components). Lincoln's work is consultancy-based, which means the brand names (Breville, Telstra Health, Dentsu) carry the credibility. But Lincoln doesn't leverage those brands visually — no logos, no "trusted by" strip. Diana doesn't need one because Indeed IS the brand.

**UX Researcher** | 🟡 IMPROVE | AI positioning
Diana positions as "AI Driven Design Systems" — specific, searchable, immediately understood. Lincoln positions as "Agentic Design Systems" — the same concept but less commonly searched. Diana also published her MCP benchmark methodology, making her the empirical authority. Lincoln's agentic positioning is conceptual where Diana's is evidenced.

Jina states speaking requirements openly: travel coverage, honorarium, Code of Conduct enforcement, diversity data. This communicates "I receive more invitations than I accept." Lincoln's site invites enquiries but sets no terms. Setting terms signals demand rather than availability.

**Visual Designer** | 🟡 IMPROVE | Site maintenance as signal
Jina's site is older, less maintained, and less technically ambitious than Lincoln's — and she still has more community authority. This confirms: the gap is not quality, it's packaging. But it also confirms the inverse: don't let Lincoln's site fall into the same neglect pattern.
